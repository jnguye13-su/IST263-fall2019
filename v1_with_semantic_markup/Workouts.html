Dryland

Different dryland excercises that help imitate different movements that you do in the water help build muscles in those certain areas allowing for more explosive movement in the water. Using different equipement such as jump ropes, medicine balls, kettle bells, and battling ropes all help you to build your muscle in the right places if you do the right excercises with them. Be creative in what excercises you do, but make sure that it somehow resembles a certain movement that you do in the water.

Weight Training

Weight training is also very important when trying to build the strength of your muscle. You still want to imitate the movements that you do in the water to increase the strength of those same muscles you use in the water. Different weight workouts you can use are pull-ups, lateral lunges, alternating dumbbell press, and squat jumps. But remember it is extremely important to work on excercises that help improve the same muscles that you are using in the water.

Taper

Taper is extremely important before you have a big competition. Taper is basically a certain of time where you rest before a competition. You are still training but not for as long or intense. With tapering before a meet it allows you to become more powerful, your stroke get a little longer, you get more fast twitchy, your arms get stronger, you feel better mentally, and you ultimately swim faster. This is proven to be the best way to prepare for your biggest meet of the season, but it is still important that you are not tapering all season because you will not be building your body during the taper period.

Swim Drills

Swim drills are extremely improtant for all different levels of swimmers. Whether you want to learn how to swim by doing different drills or you want to work on your strokes to improve your technique even more. There are hundreds of different kinds of drills that you can pick from that will best work on and improve whatever you are trying to perfect.

Hours of Swimming

Olympic swimmers train around 9 times a week for around 2-4 hours each day inside the pool. With a volume per session ranging from 4-7k. The amount of swimming that these olympic athletes do take a lot of time commitment and effort so it's not for everyone especially if you are not trying to make a career out of it. I believe it must be taken extremely seriously or else you most likely won't get much out of it.
